The Night They Came for Cartwright’s Mill

One hundred and fifty men. Forty hammers. Two deaths. The raid on Rawfolds that changed everything.

By Samuel Hartley · April 20, 1812

They gathered on the moors above Hightown as the church bells struck midnight. One hundred and fifty men, their faces blackened with soot, their hearts hardened by months of desperate poverty. They carried hammers, hatchets, and a few pistols borrowed from sympathetic yeomen. They had come to destroy the shearing frames that were destroying their livelihoods.

What happened in the next three hours at Rawfolds Mill would leave two of their number dead, dozens wounded, and the Luddite movement forever changed. This is the story of that night, told by those who survived it.

The Man Who Would Not Yield

William Cartwright was not like other mill owners. Where others had capitulated to Luddite demands—removing their shearing frames, restoring piece rates, rehiring displaced croppers—Cartwright had doubled down. He installed more machines. He fortified his mill. He armed his workers and drilled them like soldiers.

"Let them come," he reportedly told the Huddersfield magistrates. "I am ready for them."

The croppers of the West Riding knew Cartwright’s reputation. They knew he had turned Rawfolds into a fortress, with armed guards, alarm bells, and a cache of muskets. They knew an attack would be costly. But they also knew that if Cartwright succeeded—if one mill owner could defy the movement and prosper—others would follow. The shearing frames would spread. Their trade would die.

"We had tried everything. Petitions. Negotiations. Appeals to Parliament. They laughed at us. What choice remained but the hammer?"

Anonymous cropper, interviewed April 1812

The Croppers' Grievance

The cropping trade required seven years of apprenticeship to master. Croppers used massive hand-shears, weighing up to forty pounds, to trim the nap from woven cloth—a skilled operation that determined the quality of the finished fabric.

The shearing frame could do the same work in a fraction of the time, operated by unskilled workers or even children. A single machine could displace twenty trained croppers.

By April 1812, wages in the West Riding had fallen by two-thirds. Families that had lived comfortably were now destitute. The poorhouse in Huddersfield reported record admissions.

Midnight on the Moors

The men who gathered that night came from villages across the West Riding: Huddersfield, Halifax, Leeds, Brighouse, Elland. They had been summoned by secret messages passed from hand to hand, written in code, destroyed after reading. The penalty for what they planned was death.

George Mellor, a cropper from Longroyd Bridge, commanded the main body. At twenty-two, he had already spent half his life in the trade. He had watched his wages fall from thirty shillings a week to ten. He had seen fellow croppers reduced to begging. He had nothing left to lose.

The Attack Begins

At half past midnight, the column set out from the moor. They marched in military formation—Mellor had drilled them for weeks on the hillsides above Huddersfield, teaching them to move and strike as one. Their password was "Justice." Their war cry was the name of their mythical general: "Ludd!"

They reached Rawfolds at one o'clock. The mill loomed against the night sky, its windows dark, its great wooden doors barred. For a moment, the only sound was the rush of the River Spen beside the building.

Then Mellor gave the signal, and hell broke loose.

Twenty Minutes of Chaos

The Luddites attacked the door with hammers and hatchets, expecting it to give way quickly. But Cartwright had reinforced it with iron plates. The blows rang out across the valley, loud enough to wake villagers a mile away, but the door held.

Inside, Cartwright and his garrison of five soldiers and four workers took up positions at the windows. They had been waiting. At the first alarm, Cartwright himself had rung the mill bell—a signal to the cavalry stationed two miles away at Huddersfield.

Then the shooting began.

"The musket balls came thick through the door. I heard men screaming. I saw John Booth fall. But we kept swinging our hammers. What else could we do? To retreat was to surrender everything."

Testimony given at York Assizes, January 1813

The Fallen

For twenty minutes, the Luddites hammered at the door while Cartwright’s men fired down upon them. Two attackers were mortally wounded: Samuel Hartley, shot through the chest, and John Booth, struck in the leg. A third man took a ball to the shoulder but survived.

When the cavalry horn sounded in the distance, Mellor finally called the retreat. The men scattered across the moors, carrying their wounded, leaving a trail of blood that would lead investigators to a dozen villages in the weeks that followed.

Hartley died that night in a barn outside Huddersfield, attended by a surgeon who asked no questions. Booth lingered for days before succumbing to his wounds. Their graves, unmarked, lie somewhere in the West Riding hills.

The Reckoning

The failed attack on Rawfolds marked a turning point. In the weeks that followed, the government’s response intensified. More troops arrived. Informers infiltrated the movement. Arrests mounted.

By January 1813, sixty-four men stood trial at York Assizes. Seventeen were sentenced to death, including George Mellor, who went to the gallows without betraying his comrades. His last words, witnesses reported, were: "I die for justice."

Cartwright became a hero to the manufacturing class. He received a £3,000 reward from Parliament and a gold medal from the mill owners' association. He lived another thirty-seven years, dying wealthy and honored in 1849.

What Rawfolds Means

Two centuries later, the questions raised at Rawfolds remain unanswered. When technology destroys livelihoods, who bears the cost? When legal channels fail, what recourse remains? When the state protects property over people, what is the proper response?

The Luddites lost their battle. The shearing frames spread. The croppers' trade vanished within a generation. But their struggle echoes through history, invoked whenever workers face displacement, whenever progress enriches the few at the expense of the many, whenever men and women must choose between submission and resistance.

Rawfolds Mill still stands today, converted to other purposes. The river still runs beside it. And on quiet nights, some say, you can still hear the echo of hammers against iron-bound doors.

Samuel Hartley (no relation to the fallen Luddite) is a correspondent for The Northern Star covering the manufacturing districts. This report was compiled from interviews, trial transcripts, and contemporary accounts.
